🃏 Arkham Horror LCG – Homebrew Campaigns & Scenarios

Fan-made content to play while Chapter 1 is out of print · Updated May 2026

★ Best Campaigns (Ranked)

#1 · Ages Unwound by Olivia Juliet
Time travel narrative — investigators fight a cult trying to unmake time and reality. Tight, singular focus with high narrative agency.
Mechanics: 10/10 Narrative: 10/10 Fiddliness: 1/10 7 Scenarios
Why #1: Mechanics are weird without being complicated — unusual choices and trade-offs you fully understand. No rules confusion reported across the entire campaign. Narrative is restrained and brilliant: cause-and-effect time loops where you always know what's happening and why it's your fault. Only 7 scenarios — nothing wasted. The reviewer called it "maybe one of the best arkham scenarios I've played." Zero fiddliness, maximum drama.
#2 · Circus Ex Mortis by The Beard
Shub-Niggurath campaign with circus horror theming. Spooky and tense. Comes with a circus-themed investigator card expansion.
Mechanics: 9/10 Narrative: 9/10 Fiddliness: 2/10 8 Scenarios
Why #2: Moon Token mechanic is elegant — one simple rule that creates constant tension without bookkeeping. Each scenario feels fresh; encounter cards gain different context scenario-to-scenario. Story is frantic and succinct — gets to the point fast, interlaces choices with prophecy text. Bonus investigator expansion = asymmetric powers. Tight narrative + tight mechanics + your #1 "like."
#3 · Cyclopean Foundations by The Beard
Cthulhu-based campaign that feels closest to an official FFG release. Each scenario builds on a focused "Non-Euclidean" mechanic.
Mechanics: 9/10 Narrative: 7/10 Fiddliness: 2/10 8 Scenarios
Why #3: Mechanically the tightest — zero rules confusion, "smooth" is the defining word. Non-Euclidean keyword ramps elegantly from curiosity → puzzle → core challenge. Narrative starts strong (conspiracy, investigation) but takes a backseat in the second half once the mystery is solved. Still excellent — just slightly less narratively driven than #1 and #2.
#4 · Alice in Wonderland by The Beard
Journey to Wonderland to stop shadows from consuming it. Whimsical writing with clever mechanics. May be on the easier side.
Mechanics: 7/10 Narrative: 8/10 Fiddliness: 2/10 8 Scenarios
Why #4: Narrative is well-written with genuine tonal range (whimsical ↔ dark depending on choices). Mechanics are clean but simple — each scenario surfaces one gimmick. Never confusing, never boring, but rarely surprising mechanically. One weak scenario (Fool's Mate) and the finale repeats a pattern from earlier. Great story, slightly less mechanical ambition.
#5 · The War of the Worlds by The Beard
Three-scenario campaign based on the H.G. Wells novel and Orson Welles radio drama. Short, solid, no low points.
Mechanics: 7/10 Narrative: 7/10 Fiddliness: 1/10 3 Scenarios
Why #5: Solid across the board — no peaks, no valleys. Sci-fi setting (your favourite). Three scenarios = zero filler. Doesn't try anything too fancy, which means nothing breaks — but also nothing amazes. Perfect palate cleanser or weekend one-shot. Ranked last only because the others are more ambitious.

◆ Campaigns Worth Considering (with caveats)

Dark Matter by Axolotl
Deep space sci-fi horror. Wake from cryo-sleep on an abandoned ship, adventure across the solar system to prevent humanity's extinction. Spiritual sequel to Path to Carcosa.
Mechanics: 8/10 Narrative: 9/10 Fiddliness: 3/10 8 Scenarios
Why it fits you: SCI-FI SETTING — your #1 favourite. Deep space horror with strong narrative (mystery of your own memories, ancient threat). Choose-your-own-adventure middle section with branching scenario order. Comes with a Science Expansion for even more thematic player cards. Not reviewed on karkhamhorror yet so fiddliness is estimated from community feedback.
Heart of Darkness by Vinn Quest
Travel deep into the Congo aboard the Roi des Belges. By the creator of Against the Wendigo. Highest-rated campaign on Arkham Central (4.86/5, 29 votes).
Mechanics: 8/10 Narrative: 9/10 Fiddliness: 3/10 Full Campaign
Why it fits you: Same creator as Against the Wendigo (community's #1 standalone). Highest-rated campaign on Arkham Central. Strong exploration narrative. Fiddliness estimated — Vinn Quest's style is atmospheric with some translation quirks but nothing per-turn heavy.
Call of the Plaguebearer by Dr. Jack Science
Resident Evil / Reanimator inspired. Repeated use of Arkham locations builds familiarity. Complex but varied challenges.
Mechanics: 7/10 Narrative: 8/10 Fiddliness: 6/10 8 Scenarios
Caveat: Strong survival horror narrative — epic yet grounded. But every scenario introduces new mechanics requiring fresh learning. Reviewer notes "Ok, so what are we doing?" feeling after reading setup. Scenario 5 has three different location quirks at once. Finale has non-intuitive rules in the campaign guide. Great for veterans who don't mind re-reading rules each scenario.
The Colour Out of Oz by The Beard
Quest through Oz fighting the corrupting Colour. Multi-goal scenarios with Decay timers and Pervasive enemies create shifting board states.
Mechanics: 8/10 Narrative: 7/10 Fiddliness: 7/10 8 Scenarios
Caveat: Mechanics are never *confusing* — but there's a LOT to track every turn. Decay tokens count down on treacheries AND enemies. Pervasive enemies exist at multiple locations. Companions add more options to manage. Reviewer: "requires a good, methodical method to making sure you're doing everything right." This is your CPU Rule in action — you'd be running the game engine, not playing your character.
The Ghosts of Onigawa by Myriad
Set in Japan. Half brilliant quiet horror, half uneven epic scale. Good player card expansion.
Mechanics: 6/10 Narrative: 7/10 Fiddliness: 6/10 8 Scenarios
Caveat: Scenarios 1-3 and 5 are excellent quiet horror. But the campaign throws every Arkham mechanic at the wall — haunted, bless/curse, frost tokens, exploration decks — without mechanical identity. One scenario has a second encounter deck flipping global modifiers every round while multiple elites trigger end-of-turn effects. Reviewer: "difficult to keep up with." Ending is busywork.
Bloodborne: City of the Unseen by aughhh
Like the videogame — challenging, rewarding, lore-rich. Long scenarios with high difficulty.
Mechanics: ?/10 Narrative: 8/10 Fiddliness: High Long Scenarios
Risk: Lore-rich narrative is strong. But long + complex + high difficulty = survival grind territory. Repeated death/retry loops. No detailed review of mechanical polish available — which is itself a yellow flag. Only if you loved Bloodborne the videogame and accept the grind as the fantasy.

🎭 Best Standalone Scenarios (Ranked)

#1 · The Fall of the House of Usher by The Beard
Poe's gothic masterpiece. The house crumbles around you as doom ticks — locations flip to dangerous alternate sides. Race against collapse.
Mechanics: 9/10 Narrative: 9/10 Fiddliness: 2/10
Why #1: Tightly paced — doom adds damage to locations which flip over, creating a shifting map. Enemies are "fairly basic" with no complicated rules. The house IS the threat, not fiddly monster AI. Novel boss mechanic with multiple paths to participation. Best combination of mechanics + narrative + low fiddliness.
#2 · Against the Wendigo by Vinn Quest
Investigate a wendigo in the Canadian Rockies. Massive open-world scenario with exploration, weather, and multiple endings. Community's most-loved fan standalone (4.67/5, 124 votes).
Mechanics: 7/10 Narrative: 9/10 Fiddliness: 3/10
Why #2: Universally praised atmosphere — "best player-created scenario" per multiple reviewers. Open exploration with multiple paths and endings. Long (~4 hours) but narratively rich. Minor flag: originally French, some translation artifacts. Narrative carries it to #2 despite slightly lower mechanical polish.
#3 · The Invisible Man by The Beard
H.G. Wells sci-fi horror. Hunt an invisible antagonist — flip combat/evasion decks, stack them in your favour as you gather clues.
Mechanics: 9/10 Narrative: 7/10 Fiddliness: 1/10
Why #3: "Simple enough idea that ends up being a ton of fun." Reviewer: "perfect level of difficulty — hard but not frustrating." Sci-fi setting. Zero fiddliness. Narrative is good but not as rich as #1/#2.
#4 · Consternation on the Constellation by Mythos Busters
Race cultists to find an artifact aboard a sinking cruise ship. Multiple objectives, chaos bag manipulation, and a diverging climax.
Mechanics: 8/10 Narrative: 8/10 Fiddliness: 3/10
Why #4: Chaos bag manipulation is a clever twist. Multiple objectives give you agency. Ship flooding creates escalating tension. Diverging climax. Slightly higher fiddliness (large map, many moving parts) keeps it from top 3.
#5 · The Legend of Sleepy Hollow by The Beard
Take on the Headless Horseman in a haunted hollow. Multiple spread-out objectives where you choose which to tackle.
Mechanics: 8/10 Narrative: 8/10 Fiddliness: 1/10
Why #5: "Straightforward and to the point." Choose-your-own-objectives puzzle. Slow ramp of tension. Zero fiddliness. Ranked here only because it's on the easy side.
#6 · The Phantom of the Opera by The Beard
Investigate the Paris Opera House using the Concealed mechanic in new ways. The Phantom remains ever elusive.
Mechanics: 8/10 Narrative: 8/10 Fiddliness: 2/10
Why #6: Fresh twist on Concealed mechanic. Investigation-focused. "Gets the entire team planning together." Requires Scarlet Keys familiarity (narrower audience).
#7 · Delta Green: Convergence by Mr Dodger
Government conspiracy meets Lovecraft. Delta Green RPG scenario adapted for Arkham LCG.
Mechanics: 7/10 Narrative: 8/10 Fiddliness: 2/10
Why #7: Investigation-focused. Government conspiracy = strong narrative hook. X-Files meets Lovecraft. Solid but less mechanically inventive.
#8 · The Facts in the Case of M. Valdemar by The Beard
Poe short story. A man kept alive through mesmerism begins to decay. Intimate horror.
Mechanics: 7/10 Narrative: 7/10 Fiddliness: 1/10
Why #8: Intimate, contained horror. Zero fiddliness. Atmospheric dread over mechanical complexity.
#9 · The Symphony of Erich Zann by Axolotl
Stop an orchestra from playing otherworldly music. Lovecraft short story.
Mechanics: 7/10 Narrative: 7/10 Fiddliness: 2/10
Why #9: Lovecraft source material. Unique objective. Polished. Solid all around but doesn't stand out vs the rest.
#10 · Arkham Incidents (5-scenario set) by The Beard
Five short standalones, each involving an iconic ally. Varied mechanics per scenario.
Mechanics: 6/10 Narrative: 6/10 Fiddliness: 2/10
Why #10: Fun palate cleansers but deck-dependent. Best two: "Too Noble" and "Mysterious Benefits." At the narrative threshold — play if you need variety between campaign scenarios.
